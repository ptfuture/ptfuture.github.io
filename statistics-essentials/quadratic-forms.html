Quadratic Forms¶

Video lecture: https://youtu.be/2PLs3Cwt6sM

A polymonial of degree of all its terms with 2 degrees is called a Quadratic Form. When the coefficients of the quadratic form are real numbers, we call it the real quadratic form.

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} & \sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n{\alpha_{ij}X_iX_j} \\ & \sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar{X})^2 = \frac{n-1}{n}(X_1^2+X_2^2+...X_n^2) - \frac{2}{n}(X_1X_2+X_1X_3+...+X_1X_n+...+X_{n-1}X_n) \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

Theorem¶

Let \(Q=Q_1+Q_2+...+Q_{k-1}+Q_k\) where \(Q, Q_1,...Q_k\) are \(k+1\) random variables that are real quadratic forms in terms of \(n\) iid \(N(\mu, \sigma^2)\) random variables \(X_1, X_2, ... X_n\). Let \(\frac{Q}{\sigma^2}, \frac{Q_1}{\sigma^2}, ..., \frac{Q_{k-1}}{\sigma^2}\) have \(\chi^2\) distribution with \(r, r_1, ..., r_{k-1}\) degree of freedom, respectively. Let \(Q_k \ge 0\), then:

  • \(Q_1, Q_2, ..., Q_k\) are independent and hence

  • \(\frac{Q_k}{\sigma^2}\) has \(\chi^2(r_k)\) where \(r_k = r-(r_1+...+r_{k-1})\).

\(\blacksquare\)

Let \(X_{ij} \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)\) iid, \(n=ab\) and

\[\begin{split} \begin{matrix} X_{11}, X_{12}, ..., X_{1b} \\ X_{21}, X_{22}, ..., X_{2b} \\ ... \\ X_{a1}, X_{a2}, ..., X_{ab} \\ \end{matrix} \end{split}\]

\(\displaystyle \bar{X}_{..} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^bX_{ij}}{ab}\), \(\displaystyle \bar{X}_{i.} = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^bX_{ij}}{b}\), \(\displaystyle \bar{X}_{.j} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^aX_{ij}}{a}\)

\[\begin{split} \begin{aligned} \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b(X_{ij}-\bar{X}_{..})^2 =& \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b(X_{ij} - \bar{X}_{i.} + \bar{X}_{i.} - \bar{X}_{..})^2 \\ =& \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b(X_{ij} - \bar{X}_{i.})^2 + b\sum_{i=1}^a(\bar{X}_{i.} - \bar{X}_{..})^2 + 2\sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b(X_{ij}-\bar{X}_{i.})(\bar{X}_{i.}-\bar{X}_{..}) \end{aligned} \end{split}\]

But \(\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b(X_{ij}-\bar{X}_{i.})(\bar{X}_{i.}-\bar{X}_{..}) = \sum_{i=1}^a(\bar{X}_{i.}-\bar{X}_{..})(b\bar{X}_{i.}-b\bar{X}_{i.}) = 0\), therefore,

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b(X_{ij}-\bar{X}_{..})^2 =& \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b(X_{ij} - \bar{X}_{i.})^2 + b\sum_{i=1}^a(\bar{X}_{i.} - \bar{X}_{..})^2 \\ Q =& Q_1 + Q_2 (\#eq:q1q2) \end{align} \end{split}\]

Since \(Q=(ab-1)S^2\), therefore,

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{Q}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2(ab-1) (\#eq:qdist) \end{equation} \]

Because \(X_{ij}\) are independent, \(\displaystyle \frac{Q_1}{\sigma^2} = \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b\frac{(X_{ij} - \bar{X}_{i.})^2}{\sigma^2}\) is the sum of \(a\) number of independent \(\chi^2(b-1)\) r.v. \(\displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^b\frac{(X_{ij} - \bar{X}_{i.})^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2(b-1)\),

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{Q_1}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2(a(b-1)) (\#eq:q1dist) \end{equation} \]

By the Quadratic Form Theorem,

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{Q_2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2(a-1) (\#eq:q2dist) \end{equation} \]

where \(a-1 = ab-1-a(b-1)\)

With the similar reasoning:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b(X_{ij}-\bar{X}_{..})^2 =& \sum_{j=1}^b\sum_{i=1}^a(X_{ij} - \bar{X}_{.j})^2 + a\sum_{j=1}^b(\bar{X}_{.j} - \bar{X}_{..})^2 \\ Q =& Q_3 + Q_4 (\#eq:q3q4) \end{align} \end{split}\]

Where

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{Q_3}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2(b(a-1)) (\#eq:q3dist) \end{equation} \]
\[ \begin{equation} \frac{Q_4}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2(b-1) (\#eq:q4dist) \end{equation} \]

We could also rewrite \(Q\) as sum of \(Q_2, Q_4\) and \(Q_5\) as:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b(X_{ij}-\bar{X}_{..})^2 =& \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b [(\bar{X}_{i.}-\bar{X}_{..}) + (\bar{X}_{.j}-\bar{X}_{..}) + (X_{ij}-\bar{X}_{i.}-\bar{X}_{.j}+\bar{X}_{..})]^2 \notag \\ =& b\sum_{i=1}^a(\bar{X}_{i.}-\bar{X}_{..})^2 + a\sum_{j=1}^b(\bar{X}_{.j}-\bar{X}_{..})^2 \notag \\ &+ \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b(X_{ij}-\bar{X}_{i.}-\bar{X}_{.j}+\bar{X}_{..})^2 \notag \\ Q =& Q_2 + Q_4 + Q_5 (\#eq:q2q4q5) \end{align} \end{split}\]

The other terms are zero because of \(\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^a(\bar{X}_{i.}-\bar{X}_{..}) = 0\), \(\displaystyle \sum_{j=1}^b(\bar{X}_{.j}-\bar{X}_{..}) = 0\) and \(\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^a\sum_{j=1}^b(X_{ij}-\bar{X}_{i.})(\bar{X}_{i.}-\bar{X}_{..}) = \sum_{i=1}^a(\bar{X}_{i.}-\bar{X}_{..})(b\bar{X}_{i.}-b\bar{X}_{i.}) = 0\).

By the Quadratic Form theorem:

\[ \begin{equation} \frac{Q_5}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2((a-1)(b-1)) \end{equation} \]

where \((a-1)(b-1) = ab-1-(a-1)-(b-1)\).

Hypothesis testing:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} F_{24} =& \frac{Q_2/[\sigma^2(a-1)]}{Q_4/[\sigma^2(b-1)]} = \frac{Q_2/(a-1)}{Q_4/(b-1)} \sim F(a-1, b-1) \\ F_{25} =& \frac{Q_2/(a-1)}{Q_5/[(a-1)(b-1)]} \sim F(a-1, (a-1)(b-1)) \\ ... \end{align} \end{split}\]

Normal Correlation Models¶

Bivariate Normal PDF:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} f(x, y) &= \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_x\sigma_y\sqrt{1-\rho_{xy}^2}}exp\bigg\{ -\frac{1}{2(1-\rho_{xy}^2)} \bigg[ \bigg( \frac{X-\mu_x}{\sigma_x}\bigg)^2 \\ &- 2\rho_{xy}\bigg( \frac{X-\mu_x}{\sigma_x} \bigg) \bigg( \frac{Y-\mu_y}{\sigma_y} \bigg) + \bigg( \frac{Y-\mu_y}{\sigma_y} \bigg)^2 \bigg] \bigg\} \end{align} \end{split}\]

where \(\rho_{xy} = \frac{\sigma_{xy}}{\sigma_x\sigma_y}\).

Conditional PDF of \(Y|X\):

\[ f_{y|x}(Y) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma_{y|x}} exp \bigg\{ -\frac{1}{2} \bigg( \frac{Y-\alpha_{y|x}-\beta_{y|x}x}{\sigma_{y|x}} \bigg)^2 \bigg\} \]

where

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} \alpha_{y|x} &= \mu_y - \mu_x\rho_{xy}\frac{\sigma_y}{\sigma_x} \\ \beta_{y|x} &= \rho_{xy}\frac{\sigma_y}{\sigma_x} \\ \sigma_{y|x}^2 &= \sigma_{y}^2(1-\rho_{xy}^2) \end{align} \end{split}\]

The conditional pdf of \(Y|X\) is also normal with mean \(\mu[Y|X] = \alpha_{y|x}-\beta_{y|x}x\), which is a linear function of \(x\). Test of \(\rho_{xu} = 0\) vs \(\rho_{xy} \ne 0\) comes down to a linear regression hypothesis test of \(\beta_{y|x} = 0\) vs \(\beta_{y|x} \ne 0\).

It can be shown that the sample correlation coefficient is a consistent estimator of its parameter \(\rho_{xy}\),

\[ \begin{equation} \hat{r}_{xy} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar{X})(Y_i-\bar{Y})}{\sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar{X})^2} \sqrt{ \sum_{i=1}^n(Y_i-\bar{Y})^2 } } \end{equation} \]

therefore:

\[ \begin{align} \beta_{y|x} &= \rho_{xy}\frac{\sigma_y}{\sigma_x} \end{align} \]

If we estimate \(\beta_{y|x}\) by replacing \(\rho_{xy}\) with \(\hat{\rho_{xy}}\) and \(\sigma_x, \sigma_y\) by its standard sample variance \(s_x = \frac{\sum(X_i-\bar{X})^2}{n-1}\), \(s_y = \frac{\sum(Y_i-\bar{Y})^2}{n-1}\), it can be shown that

\[ \hat{\beta_{y|x}} = \frac{\sum(X_i-\bar{X})Y_i}{\sum(X_i-\bar{X})^2} \]

This is equivalent to a simple linear regression problem. If we devide it by the standard sample deviation of \(\hat{\beta_{y|x}}\) which is \(s[\hat{\beta_{y|x}}] = \sqrt{ \frac{MSE}{\sum(X_i-\bar{X})^2} }\), where \(\text{MSE} = \frac{\sum(Y_i-\hat{\alpha_{y|x}}-\hat{\beta_{y|x}}(X_i-\bar{X}))}{n-2} = \frac{\sum\big(Y_i-\bar{Y}-\frac{\sum(X_i-\bar{X})Y_i}{\sum(X_i-\bar{X})^2}(X_i-\bar{X})\big)}{n-2}\). It can be shown that it’s a t-distribution with \(n-2\) degree of freedom:

\[ t^{*} = \frac{\hat{r}_{xy}\sqrt{n-2}}{\sqrt{1-\hat{r}_{xy}^2}} \sim t(n-2) \]

  • If \(|t^{*}| \le t(1-\alpha/2; n-2)\) conclude \(\beta_{y|x} = 0\).

  • If \(|t^{*}| \gt t(1-\alpha/2; n-2)\) conclude \(\beta_{y|x} \ne 0\).
