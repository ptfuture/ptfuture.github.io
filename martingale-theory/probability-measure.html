Probability Measure¶

Probability space¶

\((\Omega,\mathcal{F},P)\) is called probability space. \(\Omega\) is called sample space. \(\mathcal{F}\) is a \(\sigma\)-field, which is a collection of sets of \(\Omega\) such that:

  1. if \(A \in \mathcal{F}\), then \(A^c \in \mathcal{F}\)

  2. if \(A_i \in \mathcal{F}\) is countable sequence of sets then \(\bigcup\limits_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i \in \mathcal{F}\)

\(P\) is a measure with \(P(\Omega) = 1\). where a measure is a non-negative countable additive set function \(\mu: \mathcal{F} \rightarrow \mathbf{R}\) with:

  1. \(\mu(A) \ge \mu(\emptyset) = 0\) for all \(A \in \mathcal{F}\) and

  2. If \(A_i \in \mathcal{F}\) is a sequence of disjoint countable sets then, \(\mu(\bigcup\limits_{i}^{\infty}A_i) = \sum\limits_{i}^{\infty} \mu(A_i)\)

Properties of \(P\):

  1. Monotonicity: If \(A \subset B\) then \(P(B-A) = P(B)-P(A) \ge 0\)

  2. Subadditivity: If \(A \subset \bigcup\limits_{n=1}^{\infty}A_n\) then \(P(A) \le \sum\limits_{n=1}^{\infty}P(A_n)\).

  3. Continuity from below: If \(A_i \uparrow A\) then \(P(A_i) \uparrow P(A)\).

  4. Continuity from above: If \(A_i \downarrow A\) then \(P(A_i) \downarrow P(A)\).

\(\sigma(\mathcal{A})\) is called the smallest \(\sigma\)-field generated by \(\mathcal{A}\). The existence of the smallest \(\sigma\)-field is because if \(\mathcal{F_i}\) are all the \(\sigma\)-fields containing \(\mathcal{A}\) then \(\bigcap\limits_{i}\mathcal{F_i}\) is the smallest \(\sigma\)-field.

Let \(\mathbf{R}\) be the real line, \(\mathcal{R}\) the Borel sets which is the smallest \(\sigma\)-field containing the open sets. \(\lambda\), the only measure on \(\mathcal{R}\) with \(\lambda((a,b])) = b-a\) for \(a \lt b\) is called the Lebesgue measure. The existence and uniqueness of Lebesgue measure is proved in the Caratheodory’s Extension Theorem.

Lebesgue measure to Probability measure: \(\Omega = (0,1)\), \(\mathcal{F} = \{A \cap (0,1): A \in \mathcal{R}\}\) and \(P(A) = \lambda(A)\) for all \(A \in \mathcal{F}\).

Definition¶

A functino \(X: \Omega \rightarrow S\) is said to be a measurable map from \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\) to \((S, \mathcal{S})\) for every \(B \in \mathcal{S}\) we have \(X^{-1}(B) = \{\omega: X(\omega) \in B\} \in \mathcal{F}\). When \((S, \mathcal{S}) = (R^d, \mathcal{B^d})\), \(X\) is called a random vector when \(d \gt 1\) and random variable when \(d = 1\). \(X\) is also called \(\mathcal{F}\)-measurable.

Theorem¶

Let \(\mathcal{A}\) be a collection of subsets from a sample space \(S\) with \(\sigma(\mathcal{A}) = \mathcal{S}\), and let \(X: \Omega \rightarrow S\) be a function. If \(\{\omega: X(\omega) \in A\} \in \mathcal{F}\) for all \(A \in \mathcal{A}\), then \(X\) is a measurable map from \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\) to \((S, \mathcal{S})\).

Proof: Let \(\mathcal{S}^\circ = \{A: {X \in A} \in \mathcal{F}\}\), note that \(\mathcal{S}^\circ \supset \mathcal{A}\). If \(A_i \in \mathcal{S}^\circ\), \(\bigcup_i\{X \in A_i\}=\{X \in \bigcup{A_i} \} \in \mathcal{F}\) indicates \(\bigcup{A_i} \in \mathcal{S}^\circ\). Similarly, if \(A \in \mathcal{S}^\circ\), \(\{X \in A\}^c = \{X \in A^c\} \in \mathcal{F}\) indicates \(A^c \in \mathcal{S}^\circ\). Therefore \(\mathcal{S}^\circ\) is a \(\sigma\)-field. \(\mathcal{S}^\circ \supset \sigma(\mathcal{A})\).

\(\blacksquare\)

It follows from the prove that, given measurable spaces \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\), \((S, \mathcal{S})\) and \(X: \Omega \rightarrow S\). The collection \(\{ \{X \in B\}: B \in \mathcal{S} \}\) is a \(\sigma\)-field. It’s called the \(\sigma\)-field generated by \(X\).

Definition¶

The probability measure \(\mu\) induced by a random variable \(X\) as \(\mu(A) = P(X \in A)\) for Borel sets \(A\) is called the distribution of \(X\). \(F(x) = P(X \le x)\) is called the distribution function.


Properties of distribution function:

  1. Nondecreasing.

  2. \(\lim_{x \rightarrow\infty}F(x) = 1\), \(\lim_{x \rightarrow -\infty}F(x) = 0\)

  3. Right continuous: \(\lim_{y \downarrow x}F(y) = F(x)\)

  4. \(F(x-) = \lim_{y \uparrow x} F(y) = P(X \lt x)\)

  5. \(P(X = x) = F(x) - F(x-)\)

Example of random variables:

  • If \(X: (\Omega, \mathcal{F}) \rightarrow (S, \mathcal{S})\), \(f: (S, \mathcal{S}) \rightarrow (T, \mathcal{T})\) are measruable maps, then \(f(X)\) is a measurable map from \((\Omega, \mathcal{F})\) to \((T, \mathcal{T})\).

  • If \(X_1, X_2...X_n\) are r.v and \(f: (\mathbf{R}^n, \mathcal{R}^n) \rightarrow (\mathbf{R}, \mathcal{R})\) is a measurable map, then \(f(X_1,...,X_n)\) is a random variable.

  • If \(X_1, X_2...X_n\) are r.v, then \(\inf\limits_{n}X_n\), \(\sup\limits_{n}X_n\), \(\limsup\limits_{n}X_n\) and \(\liminf\limits_{n}X_n\) are all random variables.

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} \{ \inf\limits_{n}X_n \lt a\} = \bigcup_n\{ X_n \lt a\} \in \mathcal{F} \\ \{ \sup\limits_{n}X_n \lt a\} = \bigcap_n\{ X_n \lt a\} \in \mathcal{F} \end{align} \end{split}\]

  • \(\lim_{n \rightarrow \infty}X_n\) exist iff the set \(\{\limsup X_n \neq \liminf X_n\}\) has probabilty 0. Equivalently, we say \(\limsup X_n = \liminf X_n\) almost surely/almost everywhere or in brief, a.s.

Limits on random variables may output infinity values, therefore, we need to extend our previous probability space to accomondate infinty values.

  • \(\mathbf{R}^{*} \equiv [-\infty, +\infty]\)

  • \(\mathcal{R}^{*}\) Borel sets generated by intervals of the form \([-\infty, a), (a, b), (b, \infty]\)

\((\mathbf{R}^{*}, \mathcal{R}^{*})\) forms a measurable space.

Integration¶

Jensen’s Inequality Suppose \(\psi\) is convex, i.e. for \(\lambda \in (0,1)\), \(x,y \in \mathbf{R}\)

\[ \begin{equation} \lambda\psi(x) + (1-\lambda)\psi(y) \ge \psi({\lambda}x+(1-\lambda)y) (\#eq:convex-ineq) \end{equation} \]

Then:

\[ \begin{equation} E(\psi(X)) \ge \psi(EX) (\#eq:jensen-ineq) \end{equation} \]

provided that both expectations exists. i.e. \(E|X| \lt \infty\) and \(E|\psi(X)| \lt \infty\).

Holder’s Inequality Let \(p,q \in [1,\infty]\) with \(\frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = 1\) then:

\[ E|XY| \le \|X\|_p\|Y\|_q \]

Where \(|X\|_r=(E|X|^r)^{(1/r)}\) for \(r \in [1,\infty)\); and \(\|X\|_\infty = \inf\{M: P(|X| \gt M)=0\}\).

Chebyshev’s inequality Suppose \(\psi: \mathbf{R}\to\mathbf{R}\) and \(\psi \ge 0\), Let \(A \in \mathcal{R}\) and let \(\alpha_A = \inf\{\psi(y): y \in A\}\):

\[ \begin{equation} \alpha_AP(X \in A) \le E(\psi(X)\mathbf{1}_{(X \in A)}) \le E(\psi(X)) (\#eq:chebyshev-ineq) \end{equation} \]

Proof: Take the integral on \(\alpha_A\mathbf{1}_{(X \in A)} \le \psi(X)\mathbf{1}_{(X \in A)} \le \psi(X)\).

Markov inequality: Make \(\psi(x) = x^2\), \(A=\{x: |x| \ge a\}\), we get Markov inequality:

\[ \begin{equation} a^2P(|X| \ge a) \le EX^2 (\#eq:markov-ineq) \end{equation} \]

Monotone convergence theorem: If \(0 \le X_n \uparrow X\) a.s. then \(EX_n \uparrow EX\).

Fatou’s lemma: If \(X_n \ge 0\), then:

\[ \begin{equation} \liminf_{n\to\infty}EX_n \ge E(\liminf_{n\to\infty}X_n) (\#eq:fatou-ineq) \end{equation} \]

Proof: If \(Y_n = \inf_{k \ge n}X_k\), then \(0 \le Y_n \uparrow Y = \liminf_{n\to\infty}X_n\), by the Monotone convergence theorem, \({\int}X_n{d\mu} \ge {\int}Y_n{d\mu} \uparrow {\int}Y{d\mu} = {\int}\liminf_{n\to\infty}X_n{d\mu}\).

\(\blacksquare\)

Dominiated convergence theorem: If \(X_n \to X\) a.s., \(|X_n| \le Y\) a.s. for all \(n\) and \(EY \lt \infty\), then \(EX_n \to EX\).

Product Measures:

Note: If can be proved that if \(\mu_1, \mu_2\) are two measures, then \(\mu_1(A)\mu_2(B)\) is also a measure. We denote such measure as \(\mu(A \times B)=\mu_1\times\mu_2(A \times B) = \mu_1(A)\mu_2(B)\) it’s a unique measure on \(\sigma(\mathcal{F_1}\times\mathcal{F_2})\)(The smallest \(\sigma\)-field generated by \(A\times B\) where \(A\in\mathcal{F_1}, B\in\mathcal{F_2}\)) such that \(\mu(A \times B) = \mu_1(A)\mu_2(B)\)

**Fubini Theorem: ** If \(f \ge 0\) or \(\int{|f|d\mu} \lt \infty\) then

\[ \int_{X{\times}Y}fd\mu = \int_{X}\int_{Y}f(x,y)\mu_1(dx)\mu_2(dy) = \int_{Y}\int_{X}f(x,y)\mu_2(dy)\mu_1(dx) \]

Corollary: If \(Y \ge 0\) and \(p \gt 0\) then:

\[ \begin{equation} E(Y^p) = \int_0^\infty{py^{p-1}P(Y \gt y)} dy \end{equation} \]

Proof: Since \(Y\) is positve, we can use Fubini theorem to exchange the integral:

\[\begin{split} \begin{align} \int_0^\infty{py^{p-1}P(Y \gt y)} dy &= \int_0^\infty \int_{\Omega}{py^{p-1}\mathbf{1}_{(Y \gt y)}}dP dy \\ &= \int_{\Omega}\int_0^Y {py^{p-1}}dydP \\ &= \int_{\Omega}Y^pdP = E(Y^p) \end{align} \end{split}\]

Theorem: Integrability implies finite almost surely¶

Let \((\Omega,\mathcal{F},\mu)\) be a measure space. If \(f\) integrable w.r.t \(\mu\), then \(f\) is finite almost surely.

Proof:

\(f\) is integrable implies: \(\int_{\Omega}|f(x)| \mu(dx) \lt C\) for some constant \(C\).

Definie \(E_n=\{x: |f(x)| > n\}\). Then \(E = \cap_n{E_n}\) is the set where \(f\) goes to infinity. We want to prove \(\mu(E) = 0\). Note that \(n\mathbf{1}_{E_n} \le |f|\), we have:

\[ n\mu(E_n) \le \int_{E_n}{|f(x)|}\mu(dx) \le \int_{\Omega}{|f(x)|}\mu(dx) \lt C \]

Since \(E \subset E_n\) for all \(n\), \(0 \le \mu(E) \le \mu(E_n) \le \frac{C}{n}\) for all \(n\). Let \(n \to \infty\), we have \(\mu(E) = 0\). i.e. \(f\) is finite almost surely.

\(\blacksquare\)

Sufficient and necessary conditions for convergence almost surely¶

Theorem¶

If \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} E[|X_n|^s] \lt \infty\) and \(s \gt 0\), then \(X_n \to 0\) a.s.

Proof:

This is an integration over positive function, by Fubini theorem, we can move expected value out of sumation: \(E[\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |X_n|^s] \lt \infty\). This implies \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |X_n|^s \lt \infty\) a.s. Further implies \(|X_n|^s \to 0\) a.s., since \(s \gt 0\), this implies \(X_n \to 0\) a.s. \(\blacksquare\)

Theorem¶

\(X_n \to 0\) a.s. iff for all \(\epsilon \gt 0\), \(P(|X_n| \gt \epsilon \text{ i.o}) = 0\)

Theorem¶

If \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}P(|X_n| \gt \epsilon) \lt \infty\) for all \(\epsilon \gt 0\), then \(X_n \to 0\) a.s.

Proof:

Method 1:

Fix \(\epsilon = 1/k\) for any positive \(k\), by the Borel-Cantelli lemma \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}P(|X_n| \gt 1/k) \lt \infty\) implies \([|X_n| \gt 1/k \text{ i.o}]\) has probability 0. Equivalently, \([|X_n| \le 1/k \text{ e.a}]\) with probability one or, another equivalent interpretation is that \(|X_n| \gt 1/k\) occurs finite number of times with probability one. Thus, \(P(\limsup_{n\to\infty} |X_n| \gt 1/k) = 0\) for all positive \(k\).

The set \(\{ \limsup_{n\to\infty}|X_n| \gt 1/k \}\) enlarges as \(k\) increases and converges to \(\{ \limsup_{n\to\infty}|X_n| \gt 0 \}\). By the continuity from below property of probability measure, we have \(0 = P(\{ \limsup_{n\to\infty}|X_n| \gt 1/k \}) \to P(\{ \limsup_{n\to\infty}|X_n| \gt 0 \})\) as \(k\to\infty\). Therefore, \(P(\{ \limsup_{n\to\infty}|X_n| \gt 0 \}) = 0\). And this impies \(X_n \to 0\) a.s. \(\blacksquare\)

Method 2:

Let \(\epsilon = 1/k\) for some positive \(k\):

\(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}P(|X_n| \gt 1/k) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}E(\mathbf{1}_{(|X_n| \gt 1/k)}) \lt \infty\).

Since \(\mathbf{1}_{(|X_n| \gt 1/k)}\) is positive, by the Fubini theorem, we could move the expection outside:

\[ E\Big(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\mathbf{1}_{(|X_n| \gt 1/k)}\Big) \lt \infty \]

Which indicates \(\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\mathbf{1}_{(|X_n| \gt 1/k)} \lt \infty\) a.s., which implies \(\mathbf{1}_{(|X_n| \gt 1/k)} \to 0\) a.s. as \(n\to\infty\), or equivalently, \(\{|X_n| \gt 1/k\}\) occurs finite number of times with probability one. And the rest of the prove will be the same as method 1. \(\blacksquare\)

Theorem¶

\(X_n \to 0\) a.s. iff for every \(\epsilon \gt 0\) we have \(P(\sup_{m\ge n}|X_m| \gt \epsilon) \to 0\) as \(n\to\infty\)

Proof:

If an element \(\omega\) such that \(X_n\not\to 0\), it’s equivalent to there exist a \(\epsilon \gt 0\) such that \(\limsup_{n\to\infty}|X_n|\gt \epsilon\). To translate it to set language we have:

\[ \{\omega: X_n \not\to 0\} \equiv \cup_{\epsilon \in \mathcal{Q^+}} \{\omega: \limsup_{n\to\infty} |X_n| \gt \epsilon\} \equiv \cup_{\epsilon \in \mathcal{Q^+}} \cap_{n} \{\omega: \sup_{m \ge n} |X_m| \gt \epsilon \} \]

\(A_{\epsilon,n} = \{\omega: \sup_{m \ge n} |X_m| \gt \epsilon \}\) decreases as \(n\to \infty\) and \(A_{\epsilon,n} \downarrow \cap_{n} A_{\epsilon,n}\). By the probability measure’s the continuity from above property, we have \(P(A_{\epsilon,n})\downarrow P(\cap_{n} A_{\epsilon,n})\).

\(\Rightarrow:\) Given \(P(X_n\not\to 0) = 0\), since \(\{\omega: X_n \not\to 0\} \supset \cap_{n} A_{\epsilon,n}\) for any \(\epsilon \in \mathcal{Q^+}\), we get \(P(\cap_{n} A_{\epsilon,n}) = 0\) and \(P(A_{\epsilon,n})\to P(\cap_{n} A_{\epsilon,n}) = 0\) for all \(\epsilon \gt 0\).

\(\Leftarrow:\) Given for every \(\epsilon \gt 0\), \(P(A_{\epsilon,n}) \to 0 = P(\cap_{n} A_{\epsilon,n})\) as \(n\to\infty\). We have: \(P(X_n\not\to 0) \le \sum_{\epsilon\in\mathcal{Q^+}}P(\cap_{n} A_{\epsilon,n}) \to 0\) as \(n\to\infty\).

\(\blacksquare\)
